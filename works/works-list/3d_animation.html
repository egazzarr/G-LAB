3D animations and narratives for the Quantum Technology Initiative (QTI), explaining quantum physics and its relation to quantum computing

Institution: CERN, Geneva

Software: Blender, DaVinci

Collaborators: Antonella del Rosso, Sofia Vallecorsa, Tim Smith

Some advantages of quantum computing are being explored to advance particle physics research at CERN. The animations were produced within the IT Department for the Quantum Technology Initiative to visually explain concepts that are hard to imagine, as they defy the physical reality we are used to. In order to understand quantum computing, the physics of quantum objects needs to be understood, and the popular YouTube videos are very often deceiving or visually unappealing. The project's objective was creating solid narratives and visual elements that can be understood without the need of a voice over, and that can be used by CERN's educational channels to explain quantum computing. Three concepts were explored: quantum superposition, entanglement and tunneling. Each topic was subdivided in three parts: a wordly example to illustrate the absurdity of quantum nature, a physics experiment, usually taught at university level, to prove the existance of the quantum phenomena, and the application of the quantum concept to computing. For example, quantum superposition is firstly explained via the famous Schrodinger's cat thought experiment, which is employed to illustrate the apparent absurd physics of a quantum object. The cat - the quantum object - is in a superposition of quantum states (alive and dead) at the same time, until the reality is checked/measured. Secondly, polarised light (the principle by which sunglasses' lenses filter light) is used as a real world example of this property. An experiment shows how diagonally polarised light is a superposition of differently polarised states (vertical and horizontal) until it is measured through polarising filters. Lastly, it is explained how the superposition of electron spin states (up and down) is what makes qbits much more powerful than classical bits in quantum computing.

3D animations for the CERN neutrino platform

Institution: CERN, Geneva

Software: Blender, DaVinci

Collaborators: Antonella del Rosso, Emanuele Villa

Video animation explaining the concept of neutrino oscillations. Neutrinos are particles with an almost impalpable mass and with negative charge energy. They come in three 'flavours': electron, tau and muon. When they travel long distances through space (for example, the distance between the Sun and the Earth), their flavour changes, based on the initial energy they are given. The Dune experiment sends beams of neutrinos from the Fermi National Accelerator Laboratory (Illinois, US) to the Sanford Underground Research Laboratory (South Dakota, US), through a distance of 1300 km, to study how neutrino oscillations work, and how the flavours change according to the initial energy. The video illustrates this concept for the visitor room at the entrance of the neutrino platform at CERN, which collaborates with the Dune experiment.
